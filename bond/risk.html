Bond Investment Risks ​

Bond Investment Risks

  • When investing in bonds, investors are exposed to the risk of unexpected losses.

  • These risks can be categorized into unsystematic risks, which can be mitigated through diversification, such as credit risk, prepayment risk, market risk, and liquidity risk.

  • There are also systematic risks, such as interest rate risk and purchasing power risk, which cannot be avoided through diversification.

Default Risk and Credit Risk ​

Default Risk and Credit Risk

  • Default risk and credit risk refer to the possibility that a bond investor may not receive some or all of the principal or interest as specified in the bond.

  • Government-issued bonds are generally considered risk-free assets. The creditworthiness of non-government issuers is typically assessed through credit ratings provided by private credit rating agencies.

Call Risk and Prepayment Risk ​

Call Risk and Prepayment Risk

  • Call risk refers to the risk that the issuer of a callable bond may demand early repayment of part or all of the bond before its maturity. This typically occurs when the issuer expects future market interest rates to fall below the bond's original coupon rate, leading them to repay high-interest bonds early and reissue bonds at lower rates.

  • As a result, investors in callable bonds may face reinvestment risk if interest rates decline, as they may be forced to reinvest at lower rates due to the issuer's early repayment.

Market Risk and Liquidity Risk ​

Market Risk and Liquidity Risk

  • Market risk and liquidity risk refer to the ease with which a bond can be traded at or near the current market price. These risks are closely related to the bond’s price and market liquidity.

  • If the market price of a bond falls below the purchase price, investors may prefer to hold the bond until maturity. Additionally, if the bond has a small issuance volume or if the market is narrow, liquidity will be limited, and the bond’s yield to maturity should be higher to compensate for the lack of liquidity.

Interest Rate Risk ​

Interest Rate Risk

  • Bond prices move inversely to changes in interest rates. In other words, when interest rates rise, bond prices fall, and when interest rates fall, bond prices rise.

  • Therefore, if an investor sells a bond before its maturity, a rise in interest rates after the bond’s purchase may result in investment losses.

Purchasing Power Risk and Inflation Risk ​

Purchasing Power Risk and Inflation Risk

  • Purchasing power risk, or inflation risk, refers to the possibility that the returns realized from a bond investment may not sufficiently compensate for the loss in purchasing power caused by rising inflation.

Reinvestment Risk ​

Reinvestment Risk

  • To achieve the yield at which the bond was purchased, any interim cash flows, such as coupon payments, must be reinvested at the same yield as the bond's purchase yield.

  • Reinvestment risk refers to the danger that these interim cash flows will be reinvested at lower interest rates, resulting in a lower overall return than the bond's original yield.

Foreign Exchange Rate Risk ​

Foreign Exchange Rate Risk

  • When investing in foreign exchange stabilization bonds (issued by the government overseas), the cash flows generated are subject to fluctuations in exchange rates, exposing the investor to foreign exchange rate risk.
