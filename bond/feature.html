Face Value ​

Principal

The face value, principal, or nominal value of a bond is the amount the issuer must pay interest on and repay at the end of the bond's term. Some structured bonds may have a repayment amount different from the face value and could be linked to the performance of certain assets.
For example, Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS) are bonds that pay interest adjusted according to inflation. The principal is adjusted for inflation, and interest is paid on this inflation-adjusted principal, helping to preserve purchasing power.

Maturity ​

Maturity

When an investor buys a plain vanilla bond, they receive periodic interest payments over the remaining maturity period and are repaid the face value at maturity. After maturity, the bond ceases to exist.

While stocks typically involve trading the same perpetual common shares, bonds issued by the same entity can have varying terms (such as different maturities, collateral, or credit enhancements), so it’s important to check the specific conditions of each bond. Even bonds from the same issuer will have different yields depending on their maturity.

Additionally, the maturity or remaining term of a bond significantly affects its price volatility in response to changes in market interest rates. This makes it a critical factor in managing portfolio volatility and is closely linked to the concepts of duration and convexity.

Coupon Rate ​

Coupon

A bond is a financial instrument that pays interest according to the terms of the contract over its remaining maturity. Typically, bonds are issued in the form of coupon bonds, which have a set coupon rate and pay interest at predetermined intervals. However, there are different interest payment structures.

A discount bond (zero-coupon bond) is issued at a price below its face value (e.g., 9,000 KRW for a bond with a face value of 10,000 KRW) and pays no periodic interest. Instead, it repays the face value at maturity, effectively providing the interest. U.S. Treasury bills (T-bills) with maturities of less than one year are discount bonds, and in South Korea, electronic short-term bonds are common examples of discount bonds.

Floating-rate bonds are debt securities where the interest rate is reset at regular intervals. The interest rate formula at issuance is usually expressed as "reference rate + spread." While the reference rate can be negotiated between the issuer and the investor, the CD rate is commonly used in many cases.

For example, a bond may be issued with the condition "3-month CD rate + 110 basis points," where 110 basis points (1.10%) is a credit risk spread determined by the market based on the issuer's risk. As the 3-month CD rate fluctuates, the interest payments reset accordingly every 3 months.

Additionally, Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS) are bonds that pay interest linked to the inflation rate. TIPS adjust the principal based on inflation and pay interest on the adjusted principal, helping to preserve purchasing power.

Credit Rating ​

Credit Quality

The creditworthiness of bonds varies depending on the issuer. South Korean treasury bonds, issued by the Minister of Strategy and Finance, are considered risk-free in the domestic capital market and are actively traded.
On the other hand, companies facing financial difficulties with low credit ratings carry a high default risk, and therefore, investors will demand higher interest rates to compensate for this risk.

Embedded Option ​

Embedded Option

A bond that includes an option allowing the issuer or investor to make specific demands, such as early repayment before maturity, is called a bond with an embedded option. The party holding the option can exercise it at any time within the period specified in the contract, and if exercised, the other party must comply.

When the issuer is granted the right to repay part or all of the bond before maturity, this is known as a call option, and the bond is referred to as a callable bond. Conversely, a put option grants the investor the right to demand early repayment before the bond's maturity.

In addition to repayment-related options, there are other types of options embedded in bonds:

  • A convertible bond gives the investor the right to convert the bond into shares of the issuing company at a predetermined ratio within a specified period.
  • An exchangeable bond also allows conversion into shares, but unlike a convertible bond, the shares are from a company other than the bond issuer.

Bonds with embedded options are more complex to value due to the inclusion of the option, and their risk-return profile differs from that of standard bonds, leading to different impacts on the portfolio.
