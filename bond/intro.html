Definition ​

Bonds

A bond is a debt security in which the issuer agrees to repay the borrowed principal along with predetermined interest to the lender or investor at a specified date. Typical plain vanilla bonds issued domestically pay contractually fixed interest at predetermined times, with government bonds usually paying interest twice a year and corporate bonds paying interest quarterly. Bonds are issued based on commercial law.

Overview of Bonds ​

Fund Raising

One method for individuals, companies, and governments to raise funds is through borrowing, which is known as debt financing. The most common way to borrow money is through a loan from a bank. However, with economic growth and the development of capital markets, major economic entities such as governments and corporations can now borrow large sums of money by issuing bonds in the capital market.

Securities

Bonds are a type of security. Securities are essentially documents representing ownership of property rights, obligations of the issuer, or entitlements for the holder. A stock (also referred to as a share) is another familiar type of security, which represents the rights and entitlements of a shareholder who has invested in a company.

Lending of Funds

When an investor buys a bond, they are effectively lending money to the bond issuer. The bond issuer (the debtor) is the party borrowing the money, while the bond investor becomes the creditor. Bonds, as securities, are traded in markets after issuance, which differentiates them from loans.

  • Asset-backed securities (ABS), based on underlying assets such as loans, can also be issued and traded in the bond market after being securitized.

Debt Securities

Bonds are debt securities where the issuer agrees to repay the principal borrowed along with the agreed interest at a predetermined date to the lender or investor. In the domestic market, plain vanilla bonds pay contractually fixed interest at predetermined intervals, with government bonds typically paying interest twice a year and corporate bonds quarterly. Bonds are issued based on commercial law.

  • Equity securities (i.e., stocks) provide dividends to shareholders, but dividends can be uncertain and irregular, depending on the company's profits and future investment plans. In contrast, debt securities (i.e., bonds) pay interest and principal according to a pre-established schedule, which is why bonds are also referred to as Fixed-Income securities. However, if the bond has floating interest rates, the amount of interest may vary.

Nature of Bonds ​

Fixed-Income Security

  • Bonds are classified as fixed-income securities because, at the time of issuance, the interest payments and principal repayment amounts the issuer must make are either predetermined or set based on specific criteria. Thus, the return on the initial investment is determined at issuance, except for changes due to fluctuations in interest rates.

Interest-Paying Security

  • Unlike stocks, bond issuers are required to pay interest regardless of whether they generate profit or not.

Time-Bound Security

  • Bonds are time-bound securities with a predetermined repayment period for principal and interest.
  • This time-bound nature of bonds makes the remaining maturity an important factor in investment decisions. Since market interest rates generally fluctuate according to economic conditions, a shorter remaining maturity significantly affects bond investment returns.

Long-Term Security

  • Bonds enable issuers to raise long-term stable funds from investors.

Repayable Security

  • Unlike stocks, bonds must be repaid as long as the issuer has the ability to repay.
