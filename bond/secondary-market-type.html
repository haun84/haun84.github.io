Types of Secondary Market

  • Secondary markets are generally classified into Direct Search Market, Broker Market, and Dealer Market, based on how investors find their trading counterparts to meet their needs.

Direct Search Market ​

Direct Search Market

  • This type of market involves investors finding their trading counterparts on their own. Individual traders bear the costs of searching for and negotiating with suitable counterparts.
  • Since the trader bears all transaction costs, they strive to reach the best price among all potential counterparts. However, it is often difficult to achieve a better price than would be obtained through a third-party market participant.
  • Bond trading in the direct search market occurs infrequently, which reduces the incentive for brokers or dealers to offer services that facilitate transactions.
  • As a result, those who wish to trade rely on word-of-mouth to find a counterpart. Advertising quotes or transaction prices is not economically feasible, so trades may occur at different prices even at the same point in time, representing a primitive form of trading.

Broker Market ​

Broker Market

  • A broker market is formed when investors seek to use an agent (broker) to find a trading counterpart.
  • If the volume of trading for a particular bond is large enough, brokers will emerge to provide specialized search services for market participants.
  • Brokers negotiate the price of the transaction on behalf of their clients by finding suitable trading counterparts, in exchange for a commission.

Dealer Market ​

Dealer Market

  • A dealer market is one in which investors trade with other market participants who are always willing to buy and sell.
  • While broker markets have advantages over direct search markets, they have the downside of not guaranteeing the quick execution of orders.
  • The uncertainty in order execution speed leads to price fluctuation risks. If the price of a bond changes while the broker is still searching for a counterpart, the client may suffer a loss.
  • However, if trading for a bond becomes active, certain market participants who wish to maintain their own buy and sell quotes for the bond will emerge. These participants are called dealers.
  • Dealers eliminate the time spent searching for a counterpart, as they hold inventory and are ready to buy and sell based on their own quotes.
  • Dealers earn profits through the spread between buying and selling prices. This provides liquidity to the market while compensating them for the risk associated with holding bonds. Currently, securities companies perform this market function.
