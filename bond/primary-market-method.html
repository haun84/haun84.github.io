Issuance Methods ​

Language

Bonds can be issued through two main methods based on the scope of targeted investors: Private Placement and Public Offering. Public offering can further be classified based on whether the issuing institution acts on behalf of the issuer and whether it assumes the risk of unsold bonds.

Private Placement ​

Private Placement

  • This is a method where the issuer directly negotiates with a small group of buyers to sell the bonds. It is usually used for issuing illiquid corporate bonds.
  • As a result, private placements generally have higher interest rates and shorter maturities compared to public bonds, allowing more flexible terms to meet the specific needs of the issuer or buyer.
  • In South Korea, private placements, particularly private convertible bonds, were active between 1989 and 1992.

Public Offering ​

Public Offering

  • This method involves issuing bonds to a large, unspecified group of investors. If the issuer or issuing company assumes the risk associated with the issuance, it is called direct issuance.
  • Indirect issuance refers to cases where an underwriter assumes the risk of unsold securities and manages the issuance process directly on behalf of the issuer.

Public Offering: Direct Issuance ​

Sales Issuance ​

Sales Issuance

  • In sales issuance, the terms of the bond (except for the issuance amount) such as maturity, interest rate, and method of principal and interest repayment are predetermined. The bonds are then sold individually to investors over a set period, and the total amount sold becomes the issuance total.

Public Tender Issuance ​

Public Tender Issuance

  • In this method, the terms of the bond are not predetermined. Instead, the price or yield is determined through a bidding process where multiple bidders submit their offers.

    1. Competitive Bidding

    1. Single-Price (Dutch) Auction * The issuing institution sets an internal target yield, and bids are accepted from the lowest yield until the issuance amount is met. * The bond is issued at a single price, with the highest accepted yield applied uniformly to all winning bids.
    2. Multiple-Price (Conventional) Auction * Bids are arranged from the lowest yield (highest price), and bonds are allocated sequentially until the issuance amount is met. * Each winning bidder purchases bonds at their individual bid yield, resulting in multiple winning prices.

    2. Non-Competitive Bidding

    • In this method, the issuance interest rate is determined based on the weighted average winning bid rate from that day's competitive bidding. Bidders only specify the quantity they wish to purchase.

Public Offering: Indirect Issuance ​

Indirect Issuance

  • To avoid the complexities and risks associated with direct issuance, companies may delegate the issuance process to intermediaries who are skilled in handling such transactions. The intermediary issues the bonds at its own risk.
  • Indirect issuance can be classified into Entrusted Offering, Residual Underwriting, and Full Underwriting, depending on who bears the risk if the bond issuance falls short of the total intended amount.

Entrusted Offering ​

Entrusted Offering

  • The issuer delegates the entire process to an underwriter (intermediary), who acts as the issuer's agent or in its own name to handle the bond offering on the issuer's behalf.
  • In this case, the issuer bears the risk if the total amount of bonds is not sold.

Residual Underwriting ​

Residual Underwriting

  • The underwriter offers and sells bonds in the issuer's name. If the total offering falls short of the intended amount, the underwriter is responsible for purchasing the remaining balance.

Full Underwriting ​

Full Underwriting

  • The underwriter purchases the entire amount of the issued bonds and then offers or sells them at its own risk.
  • The profit or loss arising from the difference between the issuance price and the offering or sale price is borne by the underwriter.
