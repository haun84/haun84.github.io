How financial markets are structured ​

A financial market is a venue in which financial transactions are systematically conducted between those who demand funds and those who supply them.

direct and indirect markets

  • Financial markets can be categorized into direct and indirect markets, depending on whether or not the transactions are performed through financial intermediaries.
  • In direct financial markets, those in need of funds raise money directly by issuing securities in their names. Some of the most common forms of such securities are stocks (equity) and bonds (debt).
  • In indirect financial markets, financial transactions flow from the suppliers of funds to the demanders through financial intermediaries such as banks.

Direct financial markets are further classified into money markets and capital markets, in line with the maturities of the financial instruments involved.

Money markets

  • Normally, money markets are where instruments with maturities of one year or less are traded, so as to smooth out economic entities’ short-term money surpluses or deficits.
  • At present, Korea’s money markets include the call market, the repurchase agreement (repo) market, the certificate of deposit (CD) market, the commercial paper (CP) market, and the short-term bond market, among others.

Capital markets

  • Capital markets, also called long-term financial markets, are where financial institutions and companies raise long-term money with one year or longer maturity periods.
  • Stock and bond markets are included herein.
  • The stock market is composed of the KOSPI, the KOSDAQ, and the KONEX markets, where listed stocks are traded, and the K-OTC market, where unlisted stocks are traded.
  • The bond market is where long-term bonds with maturities of over one year are issued and traded. The most commonly traded securities in this market are government bonds, financial bonds, corporate bonds, and special bonds.
  • Other products traded in the bond market include Monetary Stabilization Bonds, which are issued by the Bank of Korea to control market liquidity, and asset-backed securities, such as mortgage-backed securities.

Aside from the aforementioned financial markets, the foreign exchange and financial derivatives markets fall into altogether different categories due to their unique characteristics.

financial derivatives market

  • Among them, the financial derivatives market is where financial derivatives, designed to manage price fluctuation risks and credit risks, are traded.
  • There has been active trading in Korea’s financial derivatives market, mostly of equity derivatives, interest rate derivatives, and foreign exchange derivatives.
  • In particular, since the global financial crisis, this derivatives-linked securities market has grown significantly due to the search for high yield during a period of continuing low interest rates.

Financial market size ​

Korea’s financial markets have witnessed remarkable growth since the 1990s. As of the end of June 2021, the total volume of the capital and money markets was 5,662 trillion won, over 36 times greater than it was in 1990 (158 trillion won).

The ratio of this figure to the overall size of the economy (nominal GDP) increased from 83% in 1990 to 285% as of June 2021. In the same period, the ratio of financial market size to the liquidity aggregates of financial institutions (Lf) and the amount of loans from financial institutions both also increased, from 80% and 87% to 121% and 148% respectively.

On an individual market basis, the bond market and the stock market were worth 2,339 trillion won and 2,738 trillion won in June 2021, respectively, which were 67 times and 35 times their sizes at the end of 1990. They have all seen remarkable growth. Even the money markets have expanded, by a factor of 13, with a worth now totalling some 585 trillion won.

The huge growth in Korea’s financial markets is attributable to multiple factors, particularly the growth of the nation’s economy, governmental policies to promote the development of capital markets, opening them to overseas investors, improving the financial market infrastructure ever since the currency crisis of 1997, and refining financial techniques.
