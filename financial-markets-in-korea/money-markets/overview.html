What are Money markets ​

What are Money markets

  • Money markets are where economic agents trade financial instruments with maturities of one year or less to resolve their short-term funds surpluses or deficits.
  • Korea’s money markets consist of the call market, the repurchase agreement (repo) market, the Bank of Korea repo market (BOK repo), the certificate of deposit (CD) market, the commercial paper (CP) market, and the short-term bond market.
  • The institutional and legal groundwork for these markets was laid during the 1960s and 1970s, and they witnessed remarkable growth in the 1990s through a series of measures, such as interest rate liberalization, changes in the monetary policy framework, and overall reform of the financial industry.

restructured to improve their efficiency and to promote more balanced growth ​

restructured to improve their efficiency and to promote more balanced growth

  • Since the late 2000s, the money markets have been further restructured to improve their efficiency and to promote more balanced growth.
  • More specifically, limits were imposed on securities companies’ borrowings in the call market in order to reduce their reliance on the call market for short-term funding, while repo transactions between financial institutions were promoted and the short-term bond market was introduced. More recently, as the size of the repo market has been significantly expanded, various policy efforts to mitigate potential risks in the repo market have been continued.

Money markets in Korea have shown rapid and steady growth ​

Money markets in Korea have shown rapid and steady growth

  • Money markets in Korea have shown rapid and steady growth thanks to the rise in interest rate sensitivity among economic agents and to the development of short-term fund management techniques, as well as to the increase in financial assets from economic development and income growth.
  • As of end-June 2021, the money market was worth some 542 trillion won, about 5.4 times its size at the end of 2000.

Repos, CPs and short-term bonds has continued to expand ​

repos, CPs and short-term bonds has continued to expand

  • A closer look at the money markets by their sub-categories reveals that the market volume for repos, CPs and short-term bonds has continued to expand. As of end-June 2021, the repo market accounted for the biggest portion of money market transaction volume, at 43.7%, followed by the CP market at 39.0% and the short-term bond market at 10.4%. The expansion of the repo market has largely been driven by deregulation, infrastructure improvement, and call market reorganization.
  • The CP market has expanded as the issuance of asset-backed CPs (ABCPs) increased. Since its introduction in January 2013, the short-term bond market has also grown rapidly to account for nearly 10% of the total money market.

Money market size ​

Money market size

  • Meanwhile, the call market and the CD market have declined since 2010.
  • The call market has shrunk due to the imposition of call-borrowing limits on securities companies since 2010, and the exclusion of non-bank financial institutions (other than certain securities and asset management companies) from participatin in the call market since March 2015.
  • The CD market has shrunk significantly since December 2009 when it was announced that regulations would be introduced concerning loan-to-deposit ratios.
  • Accordingly, the shares of the call market and the CD market have dropped from 10.1% and 20.0% at the end of 2010 to 1.3% and 5.7% at end-June 2016, respectively
