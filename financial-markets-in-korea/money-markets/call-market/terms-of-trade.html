Call market trading conditions, participating institutions, and trading mechanism ​

Language

  • 한국어 페이지: Korean Page

Call Market Trading Conditions ​

Trading Conditions

  • Call transactions are classified into collateralized call and non-collateralized call (credit call) depending on whether collateral is provided. In practice, most transactions are non-collateralized, and there have been no collateralized call transactions since 2015. This is because the transaction procedures for collateralized calls are complex, involving collateral transfer, pledge setting, etc.
  • The maturity of call transactions can be set on a daily basis within a maximum of 90 days, but most transactions are overnight.
  • The minimum trading amount for call transactions is 100 million KRW, and the trading unit is in 100 million KRW increments. The trading rate fluctuates in increments of 0.01%.

Call Market Participating Institutions ​

Participating Institutions

  • As of the end of June 2021, institutions eligible to participate in the call market include banks, Korea Development Bank, Industrial Bank of Korea, Export-Import Bank of Korea, Primary Dealers (PD) of government bonds, securities companies that are eligible for the Bank of Korea's Open Market Operation (OMO), asset management companies, Korea Securities Finance Corporation, and money brokerage companies.

(1) Call Loan and Call Money Institutions ​

Call Loan and Call Money Institutions

  • Call loan institutions, which are the fund providers in the call market, mainly include asset management companies, domestic banks, and local branches of foreign banks (hereinafter referred to as "foreign bank branches").
    • Asset management companies manage liquidity held for potential fund redemptions by providing call loans, and domestic banks supply surplus reserve funds through call loans.
  • Call money institutions, which are the fund borrowers in the call market, include domestic banks, foreign bank branches, and securities companies (Primary Dealers and Open Market Operation (OMO) eligible institutions).
    • Domestic banks typically provide call funds, but their demand for call money to adjust reserve funds is generally higher.
    • Foreign bank branches have a weak deposit base and thus primarily obtain operating funds through head office borrowing or call money, leading them to be more borrowers than providers of call funds.
    • Securities companies, whose funding options are limited to customer deposits, repurchase agreement (RP) sales, etc., also borrow call funds to adjust mismatches in funding and operation.

(2) Brokerage Institutions ​

Brokerage Institutions

  • As of the end of June 2021, three money brokerage companies—Korea Money Brokerage Corp., Seoul Money Brokerage Services Ltd., and KIDB Money Brokerage Co., Ltd.—are engaged in call transaction brokerage.
  • These money brokerage companies primarily operate on an agency basis, and while they are allowed to act as principal brokers if necessary, in practice they almost never do.
  • Money brokerage companies receive a brokerage fee from both counterparties as compensation for providing simple brokerage services.

Trading Mechanism ​

(1) Contract Conclusion ​

Contract Conclusion

  • Call transactions are classified into brokered transactions and direct transactions depending on the contract conclusion method, with most transactions being brokered.
  • Brokered transactions involve a money brokerage company connecting fund suppliers and borrowers based on the trading conditions (interest rate, maturity, amount, etc.). The money brokerage company receives call loan and call money orders over the phone, verifies whether the transaction amount is within the credit limit of the call loan institution for the call money institution, and then concludes the transaction.
  • Direct transactions are concluded when the parties involved negotiate the trading conditions directly. Direct transactions account for less than 1% of total call transactions and are mostly conducted on a regular basis based on pre-agreed contracts between the parties.
  • Meanwhile, money brokerage companies provide real-time transaction information, including call loan and call money requests by maturity and financial sector, transaction status, and interest rate brackets, through a value-added network (VAN).

(2) Fund Settlement ​

Fund Settlement

  • The supply and repayment of funds from call transactions are mainly conducted through the Bank of Korea Financial Network (BOK-Wire+, hereinafter referred to as BOK Financial Network) call settlement system. In some cases, call funds are settled through the issuance and exchange of promissory notes.
  • If the call money institution is a bank, the settlement of call funds is conducted through the BOK Financial Network call settlement system without involving promissory note exchange. In this case, upon concluding the call transaction, the funds are transferred from the settlement-exclusive current account of the call loan institution (or its bank) to the settlement-exclusive current account of the borrowing bank, and the repayment is completed with the reverse transfer at the designated maturity time (11:05 AM).
  • When the call money institution is a securities company, the transaction often involves promissory note exchange. In this case, upon concluding the call transaction, the securities company, acting as the call money institution, issues a call promissory note to the call loan institution upon request. The call loan institution (or its bank) then supplies the call funds to the call money institution’s bank via the BOK Financial Network. For repayment, the promissory note exchange procedure is completed one business day prior to the maturity date, and on the maturity date, the funds are transferred, reflecting the net settlement of promissory notes between the call loan institution (or its bank) and the call money institution’s bank through the BOK Financial Network.
