Market trends ​

Market trends

  • Throughout the global financial crisis and institutional reorganization of the call market, the daily average outstanding amount of call transactions lessened drastically from around 30 trillion won in 2010 to 9.7 trillion in the first half of 2021.
  • Between 2008 and 2009, call trading volume declined significantly in the aftermath of the global financial crisis. On top of this, foreign bank branches reduced their amount of call borrowings in order to deleverage their assets, and securities companies had temporary difficulties in call borrowing because of the crunch in the money market. Between 2011 and 2015, call trading of non-banks declined primarily due to restrictions on call borrowing by securities companies. After 2015, call borrowing by banks also fell due to call loan limits on asset management companies and the introduction of a liquidity coverage ratio (LCR) on banks.

Looking at call transactions by lender ​

Looking at call transactions by lender

  • Looking at call transactions by lender, in the first half of 2021, asset management companies accounted for the greatest portion of transactions, at 42.8%, followed by domestic banks and then foreign bank branches, at 37.3% and 18.9%, respectively.
  • While domestic banks accounted for the largest portion until 2020, after the introduction of call loan limits on asset management companies in March 2015, their lending fell extraordinarily below that of asset management companies due to unprecedentedly relaxed financial conditions. In the case of call money, securities companies accounted for the largest portion at 37.0%, followed by domestic banks (36.8%), and foreign bank branches (21.3%)

Brokered transactions ​

Brokered transactions

  • The Bank of Korea calculates and publishes the call rate every day as a weighted average of the transaction amount for the total uncollateralized overnight transactions, including brokered transactions through three money brokerages and direct transactions between financial institutions.
  • In the period from 2008 to 2009, the call rate decreased significantly compared to the Base Rate because of the liquidity provision put into place in response to the global financial crisis. It then stabilized after 2010, fluctuating around the Base Rate level. After 2014, the call rate fell slightly to below the Base Rate and after 2018, it has again fluctuated around the Base Rate level.
