Fixed Exchange Rate System and Floating Exchange Rate System Comparison ​

The fixed exchange rate system and the floating exchange rate system are exchange rate regimes with distinct advantages and disadvantages, which significantly impact economic policy and international trade.

In this article, we will compare and analyze the differences, advantages, and disadvantages of these two exchange rate systems and their impact on the economy from various perspectives.

Fixed Exchange Rate System ​

Fixed Exchange Rate System Definition

  • The fixed exchange rate system is a system in which the government pegs its currency to a specific foreign currency or asset such as gold.
  • This provides exchange rate stability, which can have a positive effect on international trade and foreign investment. A stable exchange rate reduces the risks associated with exchange rate fluctuations, increases predictability for businesses, and promotes trade and capital movement between countries.
  • However, this stability comes with a drawback: a lack of flexibility in the economy. For example, when external shocks occur, the government must use foreign exchange reserves to defend the exchange rate, leading to a loss of autonomy in monetary policy.

The evolution of the fixed exchange rate system

  • The evolution of the fixed exchange rate system began with the gold standard from the late 19th century to the mid-20th century, followed by the Bretton Woods system after World War II.
  • The gold standard was a system in which countries pegged their currencies to gold, which served as the basis for maintaining exchange rates. While this contributed to the activation of international trade and exchange rate stability, economic growth was constrained by the limited supply of gold.
  • The Bretton Woods Agreement, signed in 1944, introduced a fixed exchange rate system centered around the U.S. dollar, and this system lasted until it collapsed in 1971 due to the Nixon Shock.
  • Subsequently, most countries gradually transitioned from fixed exchange rate systems to floating exchange rate systems.

Floating Exchange Rate System ​

Floating Exchange Rate System Definition

  • On the other hand, the floating exchange rate system is a system in which exchange rates are determined by market supply and demand.
  • This system provides greater flexibility in the economy, allowing countries to implement independent monetary policies. When external shocks occur, exchange rates adjust automatically, helping maintain economic balance.
  • However, increased exchange rate volatility can lead to greater uncertainty in trade and investment, and businesses and investors must bear the risks associated with exchange rate fluctuations. This can be a significant burden for countries that are heavily reliant on trade.

The evolution of the floating exchange rate system

  • The evolution of the floating exchange rate system began after the collapse of the Bretton Woods system in the early 1970s.
  • Major developed countries abandoned fixed exchange rates against the U.S. dollar and transitioned to floating exchange rate systems, in which exchange rates are determined by market supply and demand.
  • This transition significantly influenced the liberalization of international financial markets and the globalization of the world economy, giving countries the flexibility to conduct independent monetary policies tailored to their economic situations.
  • Since the 1990s, many developing countries have also adopted floating exchange rate systems alongside financial liberalization.

South Korea's exchange rate system ​

South Korea's exchange rate system

  • South Korea's exchange rate system has undergone several changes. In the 1950s, Korea adopted a fixed exchange rate system, where the government controlled exchange rates to maintain economic stability and currency value.
  • In the 1980s, Korea gradually transitioned to a managed floating exchange rate system, and during the 1997 Asian financial crisis, it moved to a floating exchange rate system.
  • During the financial crisis, the limitations of the fixed and managed floating exchange rate systems in responding to economic shocks became evident, leading to the adoption of the current floating exchange rate system, where exchange rates are determined by market supply and demand. As a result, Korea gained the flexibility to implement independent monetary policies and respond more effectively to changes in the global economy.

comparing fixed and floating exchange rate systems ​

comparing fixed and floating exchange rate systems

  • When comparing fixed and floating exchange rate systems, each system offers different advantages depending on economic conditions. For example, in developing countries with unstable economies and vulnerability to external shocks, a fixed exchange rate system can help ensure exchange rate stability and promote economic growth.
  • On the other hand, developed countries or economically self-sufficient countries can use a floating exchange rate system to implement flexible monetary policies and respond more effectively to internal and external changes in the economy.

In conclusion, the fixed exchange rate system provides stability but lacks flexibility, while the floating exchange rate system offers flexibility but comes with the risk of volatility. The choice of system depends on a country's economic goals and conditions, and finding an appropriate combination or compromise between the two systems can be crucial.
